ISEC-700 - Research Problem and Theory Review in Cybersecurity Management Paper, Initial Literature Analysis, and APA Reference List

 OBJECTIVE & PURPOSE:

The purpose of this assignment is to help students become familiar with the Body of Knowledge (BoK) in the vast areas of Information Security/Cybersecurity/Cybersecurity Management research, identify viable research-worthy problem, review relevant theories, understand the process of literature search, learn the difference between literature synthesis and literature annotation (annotated bibliography), learn how to follow scholarly guidelines, e.g. the American Psychological Association (APA) both for in-text citation as well as in references layout, and become familiar with the Dissertation Guide (DG) of NSU's College of Computing and Engineering (CCE).

Then...

Construct a manuscript that include the following sections:

Research-Worthy Problem Summary

The Research-Worthy Problem Summary section should include 500-words scholarly argumentation (learn what it is and how to make one in the resources above!) related to a well recognized (but not fully addressed) or emerging research-worthy problem in the context of information security, Cybersecurity Management, or cybersecurity. Start with a solid 'Statement of the Problem' that is backed by one to three solid peer-reviewed *journal* articles, followed by detailed argumentation sentences that further delineates the viability, magnitude, and impact of the problem, along with arguments for the benefits in *addressing* that research-worthy problem. Ensure all claims in this section are backed by solid peer-reviewed *journal* articles. You may incorporate one or two quote(s), especially as you introduce/define a concept or term that will be key to the discussion. Ensure quotes are taken from solid peer-reviewed *journal* articles and cited properly per APA. Excessive quotations are prohibited! Ensure your text is not an annotation of papers, rather valid argumentation, and literature synthesis.

Theory Summary

The Theory Summary section should include 1,000 words scholarly review (literature synthesis) of one key theory related risk management by uncovering the origin of the theory, discuss at least five empirical research studies that used the theory as the foundation or stream of references of major relevant work including scholarly work from other fields (peer-reviewed journals only!), and provide arguments for how such theory can be applied for risk management in the context of cybersecurity. Ensure your text is not an annotation of papers, rather valid argumentation and literature synthesis.

References

The References section should include at least 15 references of relevant peer-reviewed scholarly work. References should be taken from high peer-reviewed journals or peer-reviewed conference proceedings (e.g. IS/ACM/IEEE/IIAKM). Minimize as possible the use of conference proceedings (no more than five refs from conferences!). Be aware that you may need 40 to 50 articles in order to narrow your references to good 15 papers. Ensure all references included are cited in the text and all references cited in the text are included in the references (this is called "cross referencing").

Pay close attention to APA formatting details!!!

The References should be formatted as noted in the CCE's dissertation guide (DG) (Which is single space with one space seperation between references, NOT as per APA given that we are following the layout of a dissertation which has lots of references - make a note of that!).

Style

Also, the following guidelines should be noted:

  • Writing in the first or third person is not acceptable
  • Every claim, ask yourself - Says who? Based on what? If there is no ref to answer this, either add a reference that does OR adjust/remove the claim. However, avoid providing a "laundry list" of references in-text, so if you have a claim that has 10 or more papers supporting it, select the top two or three most relevant, or more valid, or higher quality, or most recent - depends on what's appropriate for that claim.
  • No footnotes or running heads allowed. These are not allowed per DG and also can be distracting to a reader who may only have a short time to scan an article.
  • Follow APA 7th Ed. guidelines for both in-text citation of references and reference list (two different things! Ensure you understand the difference!).
  • Ensure all numbers below 10 are noted in word layout (i.e. "five") and all numbers 10 or more are noted in numerical form (i.e. "15").
  • Ensure to follow APA 7th Ed. on proper quotes. If quoted text is 40 words or more, ensure it's noted in block-quote layout - see APA 7th Ed. for additional information.
  • Limit your conference proceedings to below four articles (or ~40% of your references)!!!
  • No use of "the author" or "the paper", "this research", "this study" allowed when referring to the work of others, cite per APA 7th Ed.. You may use "this propose research" or "this proposed study" when referring to your own work.
  • Always support your claims with references either at the end of the claim, i.e. "(Carlton & Levy, 2015)"); or at the start of the sentence, i.e. "Carlton and Levy (2015) indicated that..." or "According to Carlton and Levy (2016), ..."
  • Always use past-tense when citing from literature (i.e. "supported", "indicated", "argued", etc.)
  • Pay close attention to proper punctuation
  • Overall layout of references should be formatted as noted in the College's DG.
